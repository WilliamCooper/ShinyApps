Info for AT Hydrostatic-Equation Check

[esc to dismiss, or click the button at the bottom, or click in the main application window]

This tab tests ATX by determining an independent estimate of temperature from the hydrostatic equation. That estimate is:

ATHE = <(g/Ra )(Δz/Δ(ln p))> – 273.15

where the < > angle brackets indicate averaging, g is the acceleration of gravity, Ra is the gas constant for air, Δz is the change in geometric altitude in one second, and Δ(ln p) is the change in the natural logarithm of the pressure in one second. Individual measurements are too noisy to provide a good estimate of temperature, from this equation so estimates are compiled from entire flights or entire projects and then stratified by temperature. By default, the comparison is to ATX, but the drop-down list just above the plop includes the temperatures available for a project so any of the available temperature variables can be selected for this comparison. The height differences are those from the best GPS measurement available, and the pressure measurements are those provided by PSXC (which should have the static-defect correction from the study with LAMS applied to the measurements). The gas constant used in this equation is adjusted for humidity, but that only has an effect for cases with high humidity and high temperature. The acceleration of gravity is also adjusted for latitude and altitude. However, at present no adjustment for horizontal gradients is included. That adjustment was included in the original study as applied to HIPPO and included in the 2014 paper on LAMS, so it may be added in the future.

The top drop-down menu, flight number, and “All” checkbox in the top panel allow selection of any project and flight in the data archives. The “All” checkbox requires reading all the data files for a given project, so it will take longer (up to a minute or so before plots will appear).

Many restrictions are applied to the measurements used for this plot. The rate of climb or rate of descent was required to be in the range from 2-7.5 m/s, to avoid small changes in pressure that led to significant uncertainty in the pressure change and to avoid rapid changes that would cause the temperature sensor to lag significantly behind the true temperature. The airspeed was required to be above 130 m/s to avoid regions near the start and end of flights when flaps might be deployed. Turns were excluded also, and any periods where QCF and QCR differed by more than 2 hPa were excluded to eliminate periods where the airspeed measurement might be affected by blockage. Finally, invididual measurements having temperature differences larger than 5oC were arbitrarily excluded from the averages to avoid the large errors and uncertainties introduced by those measurements.

The number of individual measurements used for each error bar is labeled near the right side of the plot. Averages based on small numbers of measurements (e.g., < 1000) are expected to show more variation and larger errors, so these numbers should be considered when evaluating the error bars.

The mean value for all measurements is included in the title of the plot. The standard deviation listed is that of the mean; individual measurements have standard deviations of several degrees because the pressure measurements have marginal resolution for this use. The result depends on averaging of many measurements. It is expected that the mean will differ from zero by less than about 1oC for “good” projects, although individual flights may vary by more than this. If larger values are found for a project, it is worth scanning through the flights to see if individual flights are especially bad and need further attention. The 2014 LAMS paper has additional information regarding this technique, in that case as applied to the many climbs and descents from HIPPO-5, where it led to the conclusion that the calibration in use gave measurements within about 0.3oC of the measurements determined using this technique.
