The pitch maneuver

In the pitch maneuver, the pitch is varied in cycles lasting 20–30 s and the aircraft is allowed to climb and descend in response to the pitch changes while cycling above and below approximately the same average altitude. Typical changes in the vertical acceleration are about ±4 m/s2 and the approximately sinusoidal vertical motion of the aircraft extends over about 6–10 cycles. This maneuver is best if flown where there is little vertical wind and steady horizontal wind.

The maneuver has two primary purposes:

  1. If the wind-sensing system operates properly, it should produce a vertical wind that is unaffected by the vertical motion of the aircraft. The vertical wind w is the sum of the rate of climb of the aircraft (wp) and the upward component of the relative wind, and is given approximately by

Usually the most difficult measurement is that of angle of attack because it is not measured directly but instead is determined from pressure measurements at ports on the nose radome. Requiring that there be no (or insignificant) correlation between the measured vertical wind and the induced rate of climb during the pitch maneuver is a stringent test of the validity of the empirical relationship used to determine α.

  2. In addition, the relative timing among measurements affects the measurement of vertical wind during the pitch maneuver. Removing residual effects of the rate of climb on the vertical wind usually requires adjustment for the relative timing of the measurements, so this is an important check on the adjustments used to compensate for those timing errors. As commonly used, the variables entering (6) come from three different sources, each with its own clock to determine timing:

    1. The airspeed and the pressure measurements entering α are provided by the central data-recording system (NIDAS) on the research aircraft.

    2. In recent projects, the rate of climb of the aircraft has been measured by the GPS receiver.

    3. The measurement of pitch is provided by the inertial system, with measurements normally transmitted to the aircraft data system after a delay that can range up to 80 ms.

    In addition, the complete equation for the vertical wind involves additional transformations to compensate for the attitude of the aircraft, esp. the roll angle, and to determine the upward wind component relative to the Earth from the tilted coordinate system used by the aircraft. These usually have negligible effect except in turns, but those measurements have their own transmission delays that may differ from the pitch.

Because these two components of studies of pitch maneuvers are so closely related, they are combined in the QA application in a single type of plot, which shows the rate of climb with the measurement of vertical wind and lists the results of some critical comparisons:

  • The correlation coefficient between the rate of climb and the vertical wind and the influence of the rate of climb on vertical wind that is suggested by that correlation coefficient. A simple correlation coefficient is not useful because induced effects are usually significantly out-of-phase with the imposed rate of climb, so instead the peak autocorrelation coefficient for delays ranging from ±10 s is used. In addition, the tool supports adjustment of the relative timing among the measurements to determine what effects adjustments have on the performance during the pitch maneuver. An important consideration is that the relative timing corrections so determined may vary during a flight and from flight to flight; indeed, there are indications that they do. Nevertheless, these results provide guidance regarding representative values to use and the variability and uncertainty in those values.

The following examines a representative pitch maneuver. Figure 4 shows the changes in pitch and rate of climb (plotted as “dZ”). For the central part of the maneuver (5:13:15–5:16:15 UTC), the standard deviation in rate of climb was 7.2 and the standard deviation in measured vertical wind was 0.5, so the maneuver meets the long-standing criterion that less than 10% of the imposed motion should appear in the vertical wind. However, consideration of the cross-correlation, shown in Fig. 5, can refine this estimate of influence. The maximum cross-correlation is 0.44 so a better estimate of the standard deviation in measured wind produced by the maneuver is , which for this maneuver is 0.22 m/s. Although the correlation is significant and indicates a detectable effect of the maneuver, the standard deviation in WIC that is attributable to the maneuver is only about 3.1% of the magnitude of the rate of climb imposed during the maneuver.

Another meassure of the possible influence of the maneuver is to compare the standard deviation in WIC for times just before and just after the maneuver to that during the maneuver. In this case, the standard deviation for points in the interval of Fig. 4 but outside the interval listed above as the “central part” was 0.43, while that within the central part was 0.51, so this is reasonably consistent with the estimate of the effect of the maneuver in the preceding paragraph.
