Yaw Maneuvers

During yaw maneuvers, the sideslip of the aircraft is varied using rudder action in uncoordinated turns (i.e., without compensating roll) in a manner similar to the pitch maneuver, but usually resulting in a more rapid motion. Steady wind conditions are helpful. The target (impossible without some compromise) is to fly the maneuver without roll and without significant change in altitude; this requires some small adjustment to pitch during the maneuver.

Analysis of the yaw maneuver

The equations for the yaw maneuver are more complicated than those for the pitch maneuver, and the maneuver is very hard to fly. Ideally, the yaw maneuver should only change sideslip angle and heading without change in roll, altitude, or angle of attack, but that is impossible. It is practical, however, to minimize roll and change in altitude, while using some change in angle of attack to avoid loss of altitude, and that was how these maneuvers have been flown since DEEPWAVE. (Some from earlier projects had significant roll changes.) In the case of yaw maneuvers, the evaluation of the maneuver is based on the expectation that the horizontal wind remains constant during the maneuver. The calculation of the component of the wind lateral to the aircraft requires the measured sideslip, so evaluation of the yaw maneuver is based on consistency in that measured lateral component of the wind. However, the orientation of the aircraft and therefore the lateral component of the horizontal wind changes during the maneuver, so evaluation is instead based on determining the mean heading for the maneuver and then calculating the component of the wind normal to the mean heading direction during the maneuver.

As a test, SSRD was increased or decreased in magnitude by a few percent and the wind recalculated. This test indicated that reducing the magnitude of SSRD by a factor of 0.95 led to the minimum transmission (0.05 m/s), so this is a suggestion that the second sensitivity coefficient in use may be about 5% too large.

It is also possible to evaluate the effect of a time shift between the variable for heading (THDG) and the sideslip measurement from the radome (SSRD). Because, for negligible roll, the wind calculation depends on the sum of these two angles, any time-shift between them during the yaw maneuver will influence the transmission. When flown according to design, the roll angle should remain within about ±5∘, so a reasonable approximation to the direction of the relative wind is ψ +β and the effect of a time shift can be evaluated by revising the relative-wind direction to be ψ′+β instead, where ψ′is the shifted heading. Using the QA tool

Once the project is selected, the available yaw maneuvers are displayed and can be selected. Many project have none and will show only the “none” button. Once a particular maneuver is selected, the period of the yaw maneuvers and several minutes before and after the maneuvers will be displayed. There are two time intervals that can be selected, chosen using the “set time for” drop-down menu that will specify either “environment” or “yaws”. The former causes the “set interval” slider to define the period to be displayed, and the latter should be restricted to include only the period of the maneuvers so that the analysis isn’t diluted by additional periods outside the maneuver. Once the drop-down menu choice is made, the time interval can be set either by sliding the controls on the “set interval” slider or by using the mouse right-cursor to sweep over the interval desired. When the “yaws” selection is made, the selected period will be indicated by a green line in the plot.

The plot shows, as the blue line, the magnitude of the induced sideslip component of the wind. This is calculated by multiplying the sideslip angle (in radians) by the airspeed. The red line shows the lateral component of the wind, also in units of m/s. For good performance of the wind-sensing system, there should be only small variations in the red line that appear correlated with the blue line. The “transmitted component,” defined as above, will be shown in the title of the plot as both a percentage of the induced sideslip component and a standard deviation. Once the region under consideration for this calculation is restricted to the period of the maneuver, the transmitted percentage should usually be less than 10% and the transmitted standard deviation should be small in comparison to the 0.4 m/s uncertainty expected for the horizontal wind.

There are two other features of the display:

  1. The standard deviation of the perpendicular wind during the period selected for the yaws will be compared to that in the “environment,” defined as times in the selected range for the plot but excluding the period selected for the yaws. The standard deviation during the yaws will normally not be much larger than that in the environment if the yaw maneuver does not produce significant transmission to the measured wind. This ratio is written on the plot as the “standard deviation ratio”.

  2. There is another slider, called the “THDG delay” slider, that allows shifting the time of the heading by the specified number of milliseconds relative to other measurements, most significantly that of sideslip. This slider can be adjusted to determine if shifting the heading earlier or later will reduce the transmitted components. This shift is in addition to any shift imposed during original processing.
