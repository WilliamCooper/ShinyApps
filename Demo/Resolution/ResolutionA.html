Exercise Objective

The term “resolution” has many definitions, some conflicting. This exercise seeks to clarify why characterizing the resolution is difficult, and it will explain some of the varying usages of this term. An interactive display is used to clarify some of the concepts and avoid numerical evaluation of probabilities on the part of the reader. Definitions of “resolution”

There are several component contributions to the resolution, and in practice the term is often applied to these individual components as well as to their net result. The components include:

  1. The smallest difference between measurements that indicates that the corresponding measurands are different. The indication will have an associated confidence level, often 68.3% or that corresponding to one standard deviation.

  2. A primary factor influencing this aspect of resolution is the precision of the instrument. This is discussed extensively below.

  3. The smallest change in the indicator (e.g., meter or digital display) that can be perceived. For example, the instrument resolution may be limited by the ability of an operator to read an analog display or hysteresis in that display, or by the smallest incremental change reported by a digital display. In the case of a digitized result, the resolution is limited by the change corresponding to one unit in the digitized output.

  4. For a spectrometer, the smallest difference in frequency that can be resolved.

  5. For a time series, the temporal resolution is the increment between consecutive measurements, although if a particular sensor responds more slowly than this the temporal resolution of that sensor may be lower.
