Exercise Objective

The term “resolution” has many definitions, some conflicting. This exercise seeks to clarify why characterizing the resolution is difficult, and it will explain some of the varying usages of this term. An interactive display is used to clarify some of the concepts and avoid numerical evaluation of probabilities on the part of the reader. A backup document with plots but without the interactive exercise is included at this URL.

The smallest difference between measurements that indicates that the corresponding measurands are different, normally using single measurements of each measurand. The resolution will be limited primarily by the precision of the measurement. Consider first the case where two measurands x1 and x2 are the same. If measurements of each (M1 and M2) have a random error characterized by σM and the random errors in repeated measurements are independent, then the expected standard deviation arising from random error in the difference y between the two measurements is σy = σM. The two measurements will differ by less than σy in about 68.3% of cases, as is appropriate for a confidence level corresponding to one standard deviation. Figure 1 shows the probability distribution functions for the two measurements (as the blue line) and that for the difference (as the red line).

To see this case, go to the “show PDFs” tab and set the first slider to zero.

However, to find the resolution, a different limit is needed because in that case we want to find the minimum separation between measurands y′ = x1′-x2′ that leads to a statistically significant conclusion that the two are different. (Here the primed quantities refer to the measurands and unprimed quantities to the measurements.) If the two measurements are within σx we cannot conclude that the two measurands are different because equal measurands would result in a separation between measurements of less than σx in about 68.3% of cases. To find the separation between measurements required to allow us to have 68.3% confidence that the measurands are different, we need to find the minimum separation between measurands that leads to a measured difference exceeding σx in about 68.3% of cases.

Explore this using the top slider in the “show PDFs” tab, to find the separation at which the probability “P2” of finding a difference outside σx is about 68.3%. Note that, at a separation of d=σx (or a slider setting of 1), the probability is only about 43%, so at a separation between measurands equal to the precision of the sensor the probability is still far below the desired limit. You have to increase the slider to about 2 to get the desired probability. For this reason, the one-standard-deviation confidence limit for resolution is about twice the precision.

Sometimes it is desired to quote a resolution that gives confidence at the two-standard-deviation limit that the measurands are different. To find this limit, move the second slider to 2 and repeat the exercise, this time seeking P2 about equal to 0.95. You will find that the required separation between measurands is more than five times the precision.
