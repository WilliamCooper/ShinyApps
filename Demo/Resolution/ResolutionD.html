The precision of an instrument is often used as an estimate of the resolution. The “explore def. 1” tab explains that this is not appropriate for definition #1 of resolution. However, the following different definition can support this measure of resolution: The resolution can be characterized as the separation between measurements D at which two mutually exclusive hypotheses are supported with equal confidence:

  1. H1 : the two measurands are equal; and

  2. H2: the difference between the two measurands (d) is at least D.

In other words, after measuring a separation D, you would consider either the measurands to be the same or to differ by at least D with about the same confidence. The following argument allows us to evaluate the required difference D at which the two hypotheses are supported equally:

Thus, to a good approximation, a separation between measurements equal to the precision of the instrument leads to equal confidence in the conclusions that the measurands are equal or that they are different by at least the measured difference. With the above definition, using the precision as a measure of resolution is justified. However, whichever relationship to precision is used, it should be explained in specifications or instrument characteristics because otherwise there is ambiguity in just which definition of resolution is intended.
