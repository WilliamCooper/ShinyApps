Summary

The author’s preferred definition of resolution is, “the smallest difference between measurements that indicates that the corresponding measurands are different.” With this definition, if a level of confidence corresponding to one standard deviation (about 68.3%) is desired, the resolution of an instrument or sensor is about twice the precision with which the instrument makes measurements. Use the “show PDFs” tab with the first slider set to 2 and the second to 1 to see this case. If the measurement is encoded by an analog-to-digital converter, the resolution is further limited by the difference in measurand corresponding to a one-bit change in the digitized output, as illustrated in the “meaning #2” tab. This is often described as the resolution of the measurement, but this usage is incorrect unless the resolution of the digitizer is the dominant limitation on the resolution.

A different and often used definition is that the resolution is the separation D between two measurements at which the two mutually exclusive hypotheses are acceptable with equal confidence: (i) that the measurands are the same; and (ii) that the measurands differ by at least D. With this definition, the resolution is the precision of the instrument.
