Scope of This Exercise

The focus of this module is on instruments that measure pressure, so this exploration of “pressure altitude” and “geopotential height” is a diversion from the main theme. The material is included here because measurements of pressure often are used to construct weather maps for analysis or to provide input to numerical models, and these pressure fields are often expressed in geopotential height. Consider, for example, a representative “500 mb” map such as that shown at this URL (provided by the NCAR Research Applications Laboratory). The solid black lines show the heights (in decameters) where the pressure is 500 hPa. They are labeled with numbers like “548”, which would indicate that at this location the height of the 500 hPa level is 5480 m. These heights are geopotential heights, so learning about geopotential height benefits those providing data for constructing these maps and those using them. In the course of this discussion we will also review the concepts of pressure altitude and altimeter setting, as used in aviation, and the International Standard Atmosphere.

The International Standard Atmosphere

Some further simplifications lead to the form used for the International Standard Atmosphere (ISA), also described as the U. S. Standard Atmosphere, 1976. For a complete description, see this URL. It is important, and often not appreciated, that the standard atmosphere (hereafter denoted the ISA) relates pressure to geopotential height and the temperature profile is specified in terms of geopotential height. The additional simplifications are:

  1. Consider only dry air. In that case, for levels in the atmosphere below 50 km, Ma(Z) can be assumed to be a constant that will be taken as Md = 28.9644 kg Kmol-1. This exact number is specified in the definition of the ISA, even though the value for the real atmosphere is changing as CO2 increases.
