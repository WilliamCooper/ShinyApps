Sounding Explorer

The preceding tabs mostly treated the air as dry and neglected the effects of humidity. Water vapor in varying amounts is present in real cases, so it is useful to consider the magnitude of its influence. In the Temperature Module, the concept of virtual temperature was used to compensate for the change in mean molecular weight caused by water vapor. With this substitution, the equations presented for the integration of the hydrostatic equation remain valid if T v(z) is used instead of T (z) while continuing to use the dry-air molecular weight and gas constant.

This tab supports exploration of some soundings to illustrate the magnitude of the differences caused by humidity and departures from the standard atmosphere. You can select various stations from the drop-down list, and you can specify different dates. The top panels show the height and temperature profiles vs. pressure, and the bottom panel shows the differences among some possible altitude calculations. The reference for the bottom plot, the geometric altitude, is calculated for moist air and includes the latitude and height dependences of gravity. The blue line shows geopotential altitude calculated for moist air, and the green line is the corresponding result when the air is assumed to be dry. The cyan line in the bottom panel shows the pressure altitude as in the ISA (International Standard Atmosphere). The variable plotted as the red line in the bottom panel is the "d-value", defined as the difference between geopotential height and pressure altitude. It is sometimes used to construct maps of pressure fields.

Credit: The soundings have been downloaded from the weather.uwyo.edu web site maintained by the Department of Atmospheric Science, University of Wyoming. Some referenced here are already downloaded and saved in the Shiny app; others are retrieved as they are specified.

Here are some features in the soundings to note while you explore the available options:

Denver 2017/10/23:

  • (top right panel): Note the significant departure in tropospheric temperature from the ISA (International Standard Atmosphere) and the resulting displacement upward of the heights shown in the top-left panel. The bottom panel, cyan line, shows that the geometric heights are as much as 400 m above the standard-atmosphere heights. [Exercise: explain this by using the hydrostatic equation.]

Hilo 2017/10/23:

  • (top right panel) The higher moisture content at low levels produces a virtual temperature (green line) significantly higher than the temperature, and as a result the calculated geopotential heights are too low by about 60--80 m, indicated by the difference between the blue and green lines in the bottom panel. Neglecting the humidity when calculating the geopotential height thus can introduce a significant error. The sounding for Miami on this date shows a similar error, but some warm atmospheres have differences much smaller than these. (See, e.g., Albuquerque.)

Bismarck 2017/10/23:

  • This is an example of a sounding that is fairly close to the standard atmosphere. Height departures for all variables are relatively small.

Anchorage 2017/10/23:

  • This sounding has temperatures well below the standard atmosphere, and as a result the calculated altitudes are significantly above the standard atmosphere. However, the sounding has low humidity and the latitude is relatively high (about 61oN), leading to a relatively small correction for gravity, so as a result the geometric, geopotential, and dry geopotential altitude profiles have relatively small differences.
