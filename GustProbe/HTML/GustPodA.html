Five-Pressure-Port Wind Sensors

To measure the vector wind, multi-port sensors are often used. One example is the five-port sensor shown in the figure to the right. It is often used on aircraft, but it has other uses as well. This Shiny app is a demonstration of how it operates, shown in the next tab. First, this tab presents some information regarding its performance and helps a reader understand the next tab.

The five ports are distributed so that one is approximately in the forward direction, two are above and below this center line so they develop a pressure difference between them determined by the angle of attack, and two are similarly oriented on each side to be sensitive to the lateral flow at an angle called the sideslip angle. In addition, there are a ring of ports surrounding the cylinder that provide a source of air at approximately ambient pressure.

The relationship between the angles of attack and sideslip and the corresponding pressure difference between the ports are usually determined by empirical calibrations because, particularly when installed on aircraft, they are subject to flow distortion around the aircraft and sensitivity to Mach number so theoretical relationships are usually insufficient. To find the flow angle θ from the measured pressure difference between two ports on the sphere, one must also know the dynamic pressure q = 0.5ρV2. When the sensor is oriented directly into the wind, this is the difference in pressure between the forward port and the ports on the side of the cylinder, but when the flow is directed at an angle to the sensor this pressure difference also is decreased by the above formula. In the vertical plane, two measurements, Δp and Δpa constrain the two variables 0.5ρV2 and θ and allow a solution in the form θ = f(Δp, Δpa).
