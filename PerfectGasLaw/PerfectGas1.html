Instructions



Two surfaces can be selected, representing either dry air or a mixture of dry air and water vapor. In the latter case, the relative humidity can be set by the slider. The relationships are:

for dry air: p=ρRdT

for humid air: p=ρRaT

where p is the pressure, ρ the density, T the (absolute) temperature, and Rd (Ra) is the gas constant for dry air (287.0153 J kg-1 K-1) or for moist air (for which Ra=RdMd/Ma where Md is the molecular weight of dry air and Ma is the average molecular weight of the mixture of dry air and water vapor. The density in the case of humid air is lower than for dry air, so if both are selected that surface will appear below the dry-air surface but quite close to it. Despite the small difference in density, the change with increasing humidity can be important because it affects the buoyancy of the air.

The units shown are hPa for pressure, °C for temperature, and kg/m3 for density. When using the above equations, however, it is necessary to use consistent units, for example Pa for pressure and kelvin for temperature.

When a mouse-controlled pointer hovers over the plot, lines representing constant pressure, constant temperature, and constant density are drawn through the selected point. In addition, the box near the cursor displays the temperature and pressure in the top line in parentheses and two values for the air density in the bottom line. Those two values correspond to dry air and moist air at 100% relative humidity, respectively. This box can be toggled off and back on using the ribbon-shaped control near the top right of the set of controls that appear at the top of the plot (but only when your cursor is over the plot area).

There are also controls that allow you to change the orientation of the plot and zoom and pan to different parts of the plot.
