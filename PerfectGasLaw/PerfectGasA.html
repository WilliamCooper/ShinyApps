The exercise objectives:

The ideal-gas law is the basis for many important relationships among atmospheric variables. The module containing this exercise reviews measurements of temperature, so this discussion of the ideal-gas law is to some extent a diversion. However, the ideal-gas law enters measurement technology in many aspects, including the analysis of the correction to airborne thermometers for dynamic heating, the use of measurements to deduce buoyancy, the structure of the atmosphere that determines the measurement range that must be covered, and the operation of the sonic anemometer. This exercise indicates some ways that measurements of temperature in the atmosphere are used to determine derived thermodynamic properties like potential temperature that are best understood in the context of the ideal-gas law.1

This exercise has the following objectives:

  1. To provide familiarity with the nature of interactions among the pressure, temperature, and density of the air. The ideal-gas law is often stated in the form pV=nRT where p is the pressure, V the volume of a parcel, n the number of moles of air in the parcel, R the universal gas constant, and T the temperature. This is more commonly stated in atmospheric science in the equivalent form p=ρRaT where ρ is the air density (mass per volume) and Ra is the gas constant for air, defined as R/Ma where Ma is the molecular weight of "air". When necessary, this must be a weighted average of the components of the air, especially including the variable contribution from water vapor. The ideal-gas law constrains the three variables (p, T, ρ) so that, when two are specified, the third is also known.

  2. To introduce the variation in temperature associated with change in pressure of an otherwise isolated air parcel.

  3. To show how this can result in buoyancy of a parcel and how that is related to instability.

  4. To consider the normal vertical structure of the temperature of the atmosphere and how this relates to the preceding considerations.

The next tab provides an illustration of the constraint imposed by the ideal-gas law. It can be used to learn the answers to these questions:

  1. How does the density of an isolated parcel of air change as its temperature changes? [linearly, inversely, unchanged] with temperature

  2. How does the density of an isolated parcel of air change as its pressure changes? [linearly, inversely, unchanged] with pressure

  3. Is the density of humid air higher, lower, or the same as the density of dry air at the same pressure and temperature? [hint: The molecular weight of water is about 0.622 times the molecular weight of dry air.]
