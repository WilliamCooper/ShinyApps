Buoyancy

The principle discovered by Archemides is that a body immersed in fluid will experience an upward force from the surrounding fluid equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. As applied to a parcel of air, this indicates that a parcel will experience an upward force equal to the difference between the weight of the fluid it displaces and the weight of the parcel, the latter provided by gravity. If the parcel is less dense than the surrounding fluid, it will experience an upward force and will rise. The density of a parcel is related to pressure, temperature, and moisture content through the ideal-gas law, where the dependence on moisture enters through the effect of water vapor on the gas constant. This can be simplified through use of the virtual temperature, defined elsewhere in this module.

As an air parcel is heated by contact with the warm surface, some preferred locations develop temperature above their surroundings at the same pressure and so have lower density. The parcel will rise and, as it reaches levels with lower pressure, will cool from adiabatic expansion as discussed in the preceding tab. If its new density is still lower than the surrounding atmosphere, it will remain buoyant and continue to accelerate until it reaches a level where the density of the environment matches the density of the parcel. Surface heating therefore tends to lead to development of a surface boundary layer where the stratification is determined by adiabatic expansion.

If the atmosphere decreases in temperature more slowly than this, the rising parcel will become negatively buoyant and stop rising. Conversely, if the atmosphere decreases in temperature faster than this, the stratification is unstable and it will rapidly overturn to produce the expected lapse rate for adiabatic ascent. It is common to find a surface layer with this lapse rate capped on top by a rising-temperature level or "inversion" that prevents continued ascent of parcels from the surface until it is eroded by continued surface heating and associated increase in the temperature of the surface layer.

It is useful to have a measure of the varying temperature in such a surface layer, and that is provided by the "potential temperature," the temperature that any parcel in the uniformly mixed boundary layer would have if carried adiabatically to the 1000-hPa level.
