Instructions:

  1. Select the variable to plot. These are hourly observations at the DIA station for about 9.5 years. SLVP=sea-level pressure [hPa], ALTP=altimeter setting [inHg], TMCD=temperature [deg C], WD=wind direction [degrees], WS=wind speed [m/s], WE=wind from east[m/s]; WN=wind from north [m/s].

  2. Choose checkboxes for the types of variance spectra to show:

  • "spectrum" is generated by the R routine "spectrum" with smoothing as specified by "span".

  • "welchPSD" uses the Welch segmenting approach to average segments, with segment size (in days) specified by "seg l". The actual segment length is 24 times this value because the time-series contains hourly values.

  • "MEM" will show the variance spectrum calculated using the maximum-entropy approach, with "poles" determining the number of poles (degrees of freedom used for the fit) and "MEM resolution" specifying the base-10 logarithm used as the increment between successive calculations of the spectrum.

  1. The standard range in frequency is 1.e-3 to 12 (the Nyquist frequency). It can be adjusted if necessary (e.g., to focus on a limited frequency range).

  2. Set "smooth bins" to a number larger than 10 to show the spectrum smoothed into equal-size bins in the logarithm of frequency, spanning the frequency range. There are many points in this time series (83,304), so large numbers (100--500) can be used for the number of smoothing bins.

  3. Use the "Gaussian noise" entry for exploration of the effect of random error. See the next tab.

Suggested choices are:

  • For span, 3--99 (numbers must be odd; if even numbers are entered, they are changed to the next odd number). The smoothing will continue to work for larger spans, but will become slow.

  • For segment length (seg l), powers of 2; 512 is intermediate, 64 can be a reasonable low limit, values above 2048 cannot be entered because there are fewer than 4096 days in the time series.

  • For poles, 50--500; for large numbers of poles, the calculation becomes slow, so it's best not to exceed a few hundred. A small number of poles produces a smooth spectrum but will not detect fine structure.

Comments re Interpretation:

  • The small error bars at the right side of the plots, with color matching the spectrum to which they refer, are the estimated standard uncertainty in the spectrum. The estimate does not account for the reduction in uncertainty from smoothing in bins. That is shown differently, as discussed in the next bullet. For the MEM (red) spectrum, the uncertainty is usually too small to plot except for cases with very large choices for the "MEM resolution" increment. An additional red horizontal error bar shows the increment used in frequency. This is also often too small to be discerned on the plot.

  • When bin-smoothing is selected (via a non-zero entry in the "smooth bins" entry window), a semi-transparent colored ribbon will show the standard error of the averaged values. This does not consider the uncertainty of individual points as shown by the colored error bars, but the uncertainty ribbon should reflect that error bar reduced by the square root of the values averaged together unless there is real structure within the bin.

  • The diurnal cycle appears in many of these variables as sharp spikes at periods of 1 day, 1/2 day, 1/3 day, etc., reflecting the harmonics of the diurnal cycle. These may be resolved poorly unless a sufficiently low value for "MEM resolution" and a sufficiently large number of poles are used for the MEM calculation; these need to be about -3 (0.001 in frequency) and about 200, respectively, to resolve the diurnal cycle well.
