Exercise Objective

The term “resolution” is used in various ways, some conflicting. This exercise seeks to clarify why characterizing the resolution is difficult, and it will explain some of the varying usages of this term. An interactive display is used to clarify some of the concepts and avoid the need for numerical evaluation of probabilities on the part of the reader.

"Resolution" has the following meanings, depending on the context:

  1. The smallest difference between measurands that will lead to corresponding measurements indicating that the measurands are different. The desired indication will have an associated confidence level, often 68.3% or that corresponding to one standard deviation. A primary factor influencing this aspect of resolution is the precision of the instrument. This is discussed extensively in the second tab.

  2. The smallest change in the indicator (e.g., meter or digital display) that can be perceived. For example, the instrument resolution may be limited by the ability of an operator to read an analog display, by hysteresis in that display, or by the smallest incremental change reported on a digital display. In the case of a digitized result, the resolution is limited by the change corresponding to one unit in the digitized output.

  3. For a spectrometer, the smallest difference in frequency that can be resolved.

  4. For a time series, the temporal resolution is the increment between consecutive measurements, although if a particular sensor responds more slowly than this the temporal resolution of that sensor may be lower.

The first is discussed in more detail in the next two tabs, and the fourth tab discusses the second meaning. The third and fourth items are not covered in this exercise.
