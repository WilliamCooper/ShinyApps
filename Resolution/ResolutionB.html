Resolution is "the smallest difference between measurands that will result in measurements indicating that the measurands are different," normally using single measurements of each measurand. The desired indication should have a specified confidence level, typically 68.3% or that corresponding to one standard deviation. The resolution will be limited primarily by the precision of the measurement.

Consider first the case where two measurands x1 and x2 are the same. If they are measured by an instrument having one-standard-deviation random error (or instrument precision) σM and if the errors in repeated measurements are independent, then the expected standard deviation of the difference y between the two measurements, arising from random error in both, is σy = σM. The two measurements will differ by less than σy in about 68.3% of cases, as is appropriate for a confidence level corresponding to one standard deviation, but they will differ by more than σM , the instrument precision, in about 48% of cases. A measured difference of σM is therefore an unreliable indicator that the measurands are different; a measured difference of at least σM is needed to indicate that the measurands are different.

To see this case, go to the “Probability distributions, Meaning #1" tab and set the bottom-left slider to zero while leaving the "68.3%" checkbox selected.

To find the resolution, a different limit is needed. In that case we want to find the minimum separation between measurands y′ = x1′-x2′ that leads to a statistically significant likelihood that measurements will show the two measurands to be different. (Here the primed quantities refer to the measurands and unprimed quantities to the measurements.) We need measurements separated by at least σx to be confident, on the basis of measurements with precision σM , that the measurands are different. We therefore need to find the minimum separation between measurands required to give measurements separated by at least σx in at least 68.3% of cases. Then, with one-standard-deviation confidence, we can use the measurements to conclude that the measurands are different.

Explore this using the top slider in the “Probability Distributions, Meaning #1” tab, to find the separation at which the probability “P2” of finding a difference outside σx is about 68.3%. Note that, at a separation of d=σx (or a slider setting of 1), the probability is only about 43%, so at a separation between measurands equal to the precision of the sensor the probability is still far below the desired limit. You have to increase the slider to about 2 to get the desired probability. For this reason, the one-standard-deviation confidence limit for resolution is about twice the precision.

Sometimes it is desired to quote a resolution that gives confidence at the two-standard-deviation limit that the measurands are different. To find this limit, select the "95.1%" checkbox and repeat the exercise, this time seeking P2 about equal to 0.95. The integration points (red dots) will move out along the red distribution to the points corresponding to 2 . You will find that the required separation between measurands is more than five times the precision.
