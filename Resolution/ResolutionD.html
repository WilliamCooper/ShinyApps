The precision of an instrument is often used as an estimate of the resolution. The “Resolution Meaning #1” tab explains that this is not appropriate .However, the following different definition would be consistent with this measure of resolution: The separation between measurements D at which two mutually exclusive hypotheses are supported with equal confidence:

  1. H1 : the two measurands are equal; and

  2. H2: the difference between the two measurands (d) is at least D, the measured separation.

In other words, after measuring a separation D, you would consider either the measurands to be the same or to differ by at least D with about the same confidence. (While mutually exclusive, these are not complementary; both could be false if the true difference is not zero but is less than D.) The following argument allows us to evaluate the required difference D at which the two hypotheses are supported equally:

  1. If the two measurands are equal, the difference between the two corresponding measurements will have a Gaussian probability distribution having a standard deviation of σ where σ is the precision. For a separation D between measurements, the probability of measuring D or smaller separation is

  2. If the two measurements differ by D , it is harder to evaluate quantitatively what this implies about the true separation d between measurands. However, the difference between measurements will be larger than the true difference 50% of the time, whatever the measured diffeence, so we can conclude with 50% confidence that H2 is valid.

    Thus, to a good approximation, a separation between measurements equal to the precision of the instrument leads to equal confidence in the conclusions that the measurands are equal or that they are different by at least the measured difference. With the above definition, using the precision as a measure of resolution is justified. For this reason, using the precision as a measure of resolution might be justified. However, both confidence levels are low, equivalent to a coin toss. Although the precision often is used as a measure of resolution, it is usually not intended in the sense discussed in this section. Definition #1 as explored in the previous tabs is better justified. When a value for resolution is quoted, it is essential that the quantitative basis and associated confidence limit be explained in specifications or instrument characteristics because otherwise there is ambiguity regarding which definition of resolution is intended.
