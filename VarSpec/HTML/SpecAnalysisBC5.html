  2. White noise has zero autocorrelation at any non-zero lag, so the plotted autocorrelation can often be used to estimate the random error. If the zero-lag autocorrelation does not correspond to reasonable extrapolation from the first few lags, the excess at lag zero multiplied by the variance in the signal can be attributed to random error. A plot called “Allan variance” can be used to isolate the contribution from random error and also to determine over what measurement separation random error dominates the signal.5 The details are beyond the scope of this note, but the general idea is as follows. Imagine that the output from a sensor is a combination of random error (noise), signal, and systematic error that might vary during a particular sampling period. Random error can be reduced by averaging repeated measurements, while the other components are not reduced by averaging. Consider a time-series of measurements, consisting in the example below of a 26-min sequence of 25 Hz measurements of pressure from a research aircraft on the ground awaiting takeoff. Real fluctuations in pressure are small in this situation, so it is possible to analyze the signal to find the noise contribution and relate that to the precision of the sensor.

    In the plot below, the standard deviation in clusters of data assembled from the time series is shown as a function of the time interval spanned by each cluster. The minimum cluster time for a 25 Hz signal is 0.04 s, and at this point the Allan variance reaches a value of about 4×10-3 Pa2. As the cluster size increases, the Allan variance decreases linearly on this logarithmic plot, and this indicates the region in the plot where random error dominates the fluctuations in the signal and where averaging over time intervals up to about 5 s continues to reduce the Allan variance. The minimum value of of variance, about 2×10-5 , occurs at 5 s, and beyond that time averaging increases the variance so the signals with these periods are not dominated by random error.
              
    From this plot, we can conclude that, for this pressure sensor used under these conditions: (i) the random error, or instrument precision, is =0.06 hPa; (ii) the linear decrease from this point to the minimum point confirms that this region is dominated by random error, and averaging would effectively reduce the error for periods up to about 5 s; (iii) at a particular sample size, only signals with real variance larger than indicated by the decreasing part of the plot can be detected without being obscured by noise; and (iv) the minimum variance corresponds to a standard deviation of 0.004 Pa, so an optimum use of these measurements (5-s averaging) could reduce the part of the uncertainty not arising from steady systematic error to this level when conditions are steady. (The plot cannot characterize steady systematic errors like calibration errors because they do not fluctuate and so do not contribute to the variance.)

    Other points along the linear-decreasing line represent possible compromises between time resolution and uncertainty, e.g., 1-s averaging leads to an estimated contribution to uncertainty of about 0.01 Pa from random error. The part of the systematic error that varies during the sampling period with frequency 1/(5 s) is not larger than the variance at the minimum, where the standard deviation is 0.004 hPa, so this is a useful limit on at least the variable component of the systematic error. The increasing part of the plot is often dominated by real signal.
